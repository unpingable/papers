# Paper 19 — Shadow Governance — Convergence Spike

**Full title:** The System Beside the System: Shadow Governance and the Stabilization of Unauthorized Authority
**Date:** 2026-03-15
**DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.19038241

## Status: spiked 2026-03-18

## Claim Cluster
- Accumulated unauthorized promotions undergo a phase transition from isolated violations into a parallel governance structure (shadow governance) that may exercise more influence than formal rules
- A three-component stabilization ratchet (utility debt, dependency entrenchment, semantic erasure) prevents shadow governance from reverting once established
- The Potemkin layer phenomenon: formal governance persists as a legitimacy shell while shadow rules handle actual control
- Shadow governance is not inherently pathological — it is ungoverned, and the distinction matters for remediation
- Correction of shadow governance must be treated as a system migration, not a rollback

## Hits

### STRONG CONVERGENCE — Component concepts well-established

1. **Voronov, Weber, & De Clercq (2022). "Under the Radar: Institutional Drift and Non-Strategic Institutional Change." _Journal of Management Studies_ 59(3):819-842.**
   - Match type: strong convergence on drift mechanism
   - Claims matched: accumulated deviations becoming institutional change; non-strategic (not deliberate) transformation of institutional order
   - How close: Directly theorizes how minor practice deviations, when tolerated/normalized, reshape institutional order incrementally. The "under the radar" framing parallels the paper's claim that shadow governance emerges from accumulated unauthorized promotions. However, Voronov et al. do not model a phase transition, a stabilization ratchet, or the Potemkin layer. They describe drift as continuous, not as undergoing a qualitative shift into a parallel governance structure.
   - Gap: No phase-transition threshold, no stabilization mechanism, no formal/informal decoupling model.

2. **Vaughan, Diane (1996/ongoing). Normalization of Deviance.**
   - Match type: strong convergence on the input mechanism
   - Claims matched: deviance from rules becomes culturally normalized; incremental drift away from standards
   - How close: Foundational concept. Paper 19's "accumulated unauthorized promotions" directly builds on Vaughan's normalization of deviance. Recent applications (2025) extend this to AI safety contexts (Embracethered blog) and military contexts (US Army, 2024). However, Vaughan treats normalization as a failure mode leading to catastrophe, not as a transition into a stable parallel governance structure.
   - Gap: Vaughan's framework is catastrophe-oriented, not governance-oriented. No model of stabilization or persistence as a system.

3. **Meyer & Rowan (1977/ongoing). "Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure as Myth and Ceremony."**
   - Match type: strong convergence on the Potemkin layer
   - Claims matched: formal structures persist ceremonially while actual practices diverge; decoupling of formal structure from operational reality
   - How close: The "decoupling" concept is essentially the Potemkin layer by another name. Organizations adopt formal structures for legitimacy while actual work follows different rules. This is the most direct precursor to Paper 19's Potemkin layer claim. Still widely cited in 2023-2025 institutional theory literature.
   - Gap: Meyer & Rowan describe decoupling as a coping strategy for legitimacy, not as a specific phase in the lifecycle of shadow governance. No mechanism for how decoupling emerges from accumulated deviations, and no stabilization ratchet.

4. **Higgs, Robert (1987/ongoing). The Ratchet Effect. Extended by Coyne, Hall & Yonk (2025) "Extensions and Expansions of the Ratchet Effect," _The Independent Review_ 29(4), Spring 2025 symposium.**
   - Match type: strong convergence on one-way authority accumulation
   - Claims matched: authority expansion is irreversible (ratchet); government never fully returns to pre-crisis scope
   - How close: The ratchet effect directly parallels Paper 19's stabilization ratchet — authority accumulates in one direction and resists rollback. The 2025 symposium extends this to nuclear weapons policy, healthcare, and China, demonstrating broad applicability. However, Higgs frames this as government growth during crises, not as informal/shadow governance displacing formal governance. The mechanism is crisis-driven expansion of formal authority, not the stabilization of unauthorized informal authority.
   - Gap: Higgs's ratchet is about formal government expansion, not informal shadow governance. No utility debt, dependency entrenchment, or semantic erasure components. No Potemkin layer.

5. **Fukuyama, Francis (2014/ongoing). _Political Order and Political Decay._**
   - Match type: strong convergence on decay and informal replacement
   - Claims matched: institutional decay where informal authority (clientelism, patronage) replaces formal institutional function; "empty mimicry" where states take on formal appearance while functioning as patron-client networks
   - How close: Fukuyama's "empty mimicry" is strikingly close to the Potemkin layer. His analysis of institutional rigidity and repatrimonialization converges with the claim that shadow governance resists correction. However, Fukuyama works at the level of political systems and state-building, not organizational governance. His framework is historical-comparative, not mechanistic.
   - Gap: No phase-transition model, no three-component stabilization ratchet, no system-migration remediation prescription.

### MODERATE CONVERGENCE — Parallel territory, different framing

6. **Rich, Jessica A. J. (2023). "Outsourcing Bureaucracy to Evade Accountability: How Public Servants Build Shadow State Capacity." _American Political Science Review_ 117(3):835-850.**
   - Match type: moderate convergence
   - Claims matched: public servants create "shadow" state capacity outside formal accountability; shadow capacity can produce effective outcomes (ungoverned, not pathological)
   - How close: Rich directly uses the term "shadow state capacity" and shows that informal workarounds can produce "pockets of effectiveness" — converging with Paper 19's claim that shadow governance is ungoverned rather than inherently pathological. Based on Brazilian social programs. However, Rich frames this as deliberate outsourcing to evade accountability rules, not as emergent phase transition from accumulated deviations.
   - Gap: No emergence mechanism, no stabilization ratchet, no Potemkin layer. The shadow capacity is deliberately constructed, not emergently stabilized.

7. **Furstenau, Rothe & Sandner (2017). "Shadow Systems, Risk, and Shifting Power Relations in Organizations." _Communications of the AIS_ 41(3).**
   - Match type: moderate convergence
   - Claims matched: shadow IT systems persist despite formal programs to eliminate them; power shifts between business units and central IT; shadow systems become entrenched through dependency
   - How close: Identifies two feedback cycles — one creating shadow systems (cost/time pressure), one opposing them (risk perception). The entrenchment of shadow IT through dependency directly parallels Paper 19's dependency entrenchment component. However, published 2017; focused on IT systems specifically, not governance structures broadly.
   - Gap: No phase transition, no Potemkin layer, no semantic erasure. Models shadow systems as IT-specific, not as a general governance phenomenon.

8. **Shadow AI Literature (2024-2025): ISACA, CIO, IEEE Computer Society, multiple industry sources.**
   - Match type: moderate convergence (empirical parallel)
   - Claims matched: unauthorized AI tools become entrenched; 60%+ employees use AI without governance; formal policies exist as shells while actual AI usage is ungoverned; 80-90% of apps unknown to IT
   - How close: Shadow AI is a live, large-scale empirical instance of Paper 19's claims playing out in real time. GenAI traffic surged 890% in 2024; only 37% of organizations have governance policies. The formal-informal gap is exactly the Potemkin layer. The dependency on shadow AI tools that organizations cannot remove without disrupting operations is exactly utility debt and dependency entrenchment.
   - Gap: Industry literature describes the phenomenon but does not theorize it as a phase transition or stabilization ratchet. No formal model. Treated as a security/compliance problem, not a governance theory problem.

9. **Anderson, Rick (2025). "In the Shadow of Bureaucratic Governance: A Framework for Internal Governance in Government Organisations." _Australian Journal of Public Administration._**
   - Match type: moderate convergence
   - Claims matched: internal governance structures interact with and may conflict with external network governance; bureaucratic governance can clash with network governance
   - How close: Examines how internal governance modes affect organizational contributions to external networks, identifying tensions between bureaucratic and network governance forms. Touches on shadow dynamics but from a design perspective rather than an emergence/drift perspective.
   - Gap: Prescriptive framework, not an emergence model. No phase transition, no stabilization ratchet.

10. **Shadow Management (Obi et al., 2025). "Shadow Management: An Analysis of How Informal Power Structures Influence Decision-Making in Today's Organisations." _IJSSMR_ 11(8):165-178.**
    - Match type: moderate convergence
    - Claims matched: informal power structures significantly influence organizational decision-making; shadow management moderates the relationship between informal power and decisions
    - How close: Directly names "shadow management" as a moderating variable and recommends organizations "systematically recognize and integrate shadow management analysis into strategic assessments." This is close to Paper 19's framing of shadow governance as real governance requiring recognition, not elimination. However, empirical scope is narrow and theoretical framework (RBV, Agency Theory) differs.
    - Gap: No emergence mechanism, no stabilization ratchet, no Potemkin layer, no system-migration remediation model.

11. **Heritier, Adrienne (2002/ongoing). "The Shadow of Hierarchy and New Modes of Governance."**
    - Match type: moderate convergence
    - Claims matched: informal/networked governance operates in the "shadow" of formal hierarchical authority; formal authority shapes informal governance even when not directly exercised
    - How close: Heritier's concept of governance operating "in the shadow of hierarchy" is the inverse of Paper 19's claim — she argues that informal governance is shaped by formal authority lurking in the background, whereas Paper 19 argues that informal governance can displace formal authority. Both recognize the formal-informal interaction but from opposite directions.
    - Gap: Opposite causal direction. No phase transition, no stabilization ratchet.

    - Claims matched: 27% of S&P 500 executives participate in informal advisory circles influencing major decisions; shadow boards lack formal charter or SEC reporting
    - How close: Describes informal corporate power structures but frames them as deliberate elite coordination, not emergent drift from accumulated deviations.

13. **Autocratic Parallel Structures (Democratic Erosion literature, 2023-2024).**

    - Claims matched: autocrats rule via parallel structures while maintaining a "veneer of innocence"; formal institutions hollowed while informal control persists
    - How close: Describes the Potemkin layer at state level but in the context of deliberate autocratic strategy, not emergent organizational dynamics.

14. **Nepal Federal Governance (2026). Informal governance in hybrid federalism.**
    - Match type: weak convergence
    - Claims matched: federalism entrenches central dominance through informal mechanisms despite formal institutional reforms
    - How close: Shows how formal institutional change can paradoxically entrench informal power — relevant to the claim that rollback fails.

## Notes

**What converges strongly:**
- The input mechanism (normalization of deviance / institutional drift) is well-established (Vaughan 1996, Voronov et al. 2022).
- The Potemkin layer has a direct precursor in Meyer & Rowan's decoupling (1977), Fukuyama's "empty mimicry," and is empirically visible in the shadow AI phenomenon (2024-2025).
- The one-way ratchet / irreversibility of authority accumulation is well-theorized (Higgs 1987, Coyne/Hall 2025 symposium).
- The "ungoverned, not pathological" framing has a parallel in Rich (2023) on shadow state capacity producing "pockets of effectiveness."

**What remains novel in Paper 19:**
1. **The phase-transition claim** — that accumulated deviations undergo a qualitative shift (not just continuous drift) into a structurally distinct parallel governance system. No prior work models this as a phase transition with a threshold.
2. **The three-component stabilization ratchet** (utility debt, dependency entrenchment, semantic erasure) as a unified mechanism. While dependency entrenchment has parallels in shadow IT/AI literature, "utility debt" and "semantic erasure" as named concepts appear to be original. The combination as a self-reinforcing triple lock is novel.
3. **The explicit Potemkin layer as a named phase** in shadow governance lifecycle, rather than just a general institutional phenomenon.
4. **The system-migration remediation prescription** — that correction must be treated as migration rather than rollback. While the ratchet effect literature documents why rollback fails, no prior work prescribes system migration as the alternative remediation strategy for shadow governance specifically.
5. **The unified lifecycle model** — connecting normalization of deviance -> phase transition -> stabilization ratchet -> Potemkin layer -> system migration. Each component has precursors; the integrated lifecycle is original.

**Potential challenge to novelty:**
The shadow AI/IT literature (2024-2025) is converging rapidly on many of these observations empirically, but without the theoretical apparatus. If someone in that community formalizes what they are observing, it could independently arrive at similar conclusions. The window for theoretical priority on the lifecycle model is narrowing.

## Verdict

**Partial convergence confirmed, as expected.** The component concepts (normalization of deviance, decoupling, ratchet effect, shadow systems) are well-established across institutional theory, political science, and organizational sociology. Paper 19's contribution is the synthesis: (1) modeling the transition from drift to parallel governance as a phase transition, (2) the three-component stabilization ratchet as a unified mechanism, (3) the Potemkin layer as a named lifecycle phase, and (4) the system-migration remediation prescription. These integrative and mechanistic contributions appear to have no direct prior equivalent in the literature surveyed. The shadow AI phenomenon (2024-2025) provides a strong empirical validation case that the field has not yet theorized at the level Paper 19 proposes.
