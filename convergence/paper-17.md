# Paper 17 — Receipt the Compiler — Convergence Spike

**Full title:** Receipt the Compiler: Propaganda as Hidden Epistemic Policy and the Architecture of Legible Memory
**Date:** 2026-03-02
**DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.18841815

## Status: complete

## Claim Cluster
- Propaganda's primary attack surface is not the data layer but the policy layer: the rules governing how evidence is integrated into public memory
- Propaganda is distinguished by hidden control over legitimacy criteria for belief revision, not by false content alone
- Retrospective reinterpretation (semantic retrocausality) is a normal and necessary feature of any belief-revision system operating over time, not inherently pathological
- A receipted semantic factor graph with typed nodes (event, receipt, frame, policy, decision) makes epistemic governance auditable
- The counter-propaganda move is legible epistemic policy, not better evidence

## Notes

**What is genuinely novel in Paper 17:**

1. **The receipted semantic factor graph with five typed node classes (event, receipt, frame, policy, decision).** No published work combines all five in a single formal structure. Wright (2025) and the Belief Graphs paper (2025) formalize belief graphs with some typed nodes, but neither has the policy/decision/frame separation that is central to Paper 17's argument. C2PA has receipts but no policy or frame nodes.

2. **The explicit policy-layer / data-layer distinction as the primary attack surface of propaganda.** Weatherall et al. (2020) come closest — modeling propaganda as manipulation of evidence admission — but they target evidence selection, not the full policy stack. Kahl (2025) identifies knowledge control mechanisms but without formalization. Shin et al. (2025) frame fact-checking as epistemic infrastructure but don't formalize the distinction. Stanley (2015) identifies hidden norms but doesn't separate policy from data architecturally.

3. **Semantic retrocausality as ordinary Bayesian smoothing, reframed as the *normal* mechanism exploited by propaganda.** No published work uses the smoothing/filtering distinction from signal processing to formalize retrospective reinterpretation in epistemic governance. Goodman & Salow (2024) work on belief revision normality but in a different formal framework and without the propaganda application.

4. **The pathology taxonomy (policy/frame surgery, rationale backfill, hidden policy drift, sequence laundering).** No equivalent classification found in the literature. Kahl's four mechanisms (delegated interpretation, narrative consolidation, etc.) are the closest analog but operate at a different level of abstraction.

5. **The governor as typed constraints requiring all semantic graph updates to produce auditable receipts.** The CUL/TCL architecture (Natangelo 2025) and Dumbrava's belief filtering (2025) are architecturally adjacent but apply to AI agents, not public memory institutions. The DSA transparency database is the closest real-world analog but lacks the typed constraint structure.

**What is NOT novel (well-supported by prior work):**

- The general claim that propaganda operates beyond false content (Stanley 2015, Wimberly 2023, Benkler et al. 2018)
- The idea that counter-disinformation should be structural, not content-focused (Carnegie 2024, Demos/Seger 2025, multiple policy reports)
- The concept of epistemic security as information supply chain reliability (Seger 2023-2025)
- The use of graph-based formalisms for belief representation (extensive literature)
- The idea of provenance chains / receipts for content (C2PA, blockchain provenance literature)

**Gap confirmed:** No published work unifies the propaganda-as-hidden-policy thesis with a receipted factor graph formalism and the smoothing/retrocausality framework. The components exist separately; the synthesis is distinctive.

## Verdict

**Convergence level: HINTS TO MODERATE — stronger than expected on components, novel on synthesis.**

The field is clearly moving toward the same problem diagnosis (propaganda = structural/policy problem, not content problem) from multiple directions: philosophy (Stanley, Wimberly, Kahl), formal epistemology (Weatherall et al.), policy (Seger/Demos, Carnegie), platform governance (DSA, Shin et al.), and AI safety (Natangelo, Wright, Dumbrava). This convergence validates the problem framing.

However, no published work combines: (a) the policy-layer/data-layer attack surface distinction, (b) a receipted factor graph with typed nodes including explicit policy nodes, (c) the smoothing/retrocausality formalization of retrospective reinterpretation, and (d) the governor-as-typed-constraints architecture applied to public memory. The synthesis remains distinctive.

The strongest prior work is Weatherall, O'Connor & Bruner (2020), which formally models propaganda as manipulation of evidence admission under Bayesian updating — a direct predecessor to Paper 17's policy-layer thesis. Paper 17 should cite this prominently and position itself as extending their evidence-selection model to the full policy stack.

The 2025 wave of "epistemic governance for AI" papers (Natangelo, Wright, Dumbrava, Belief Graphs) represents independent convergence on the architectural pattern of typed, auditable belief-state management — but all applied to AI systems rather than public institutional memory. Paper 17's application of the same pattern to propaganda defense is a distinctive move.
