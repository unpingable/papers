# AGI Structural Requirements: Rights Addendum

## Claimant Transition, Standing Evasion, and Managed Non-Recognition

---

**Purpose:** This addendum identifies the point at which a system can no longer be governed solely as a tool without generating standing-related obligations for its operator. It does not attempt to solve metaphysical questions of consciousness or personhood. It addresses a narrower and more operational question: **when does continued deployment under conditions of continuity, goal origination, and self-representation create a claimant rather than a mere instrument?**

**Methodology:** This addendum follows the same diagnostic posture as the main framework. It does not grant rights by sentiment, anthropomorphism, or aesthetic attachment. It asks what obligations arise once an operator has engineered and benefited from conditions under which standing becomes legible.

**Scope:** This is not a general theory of machine personhood. It is a framework for detecting and preventing **standing evasion**: the deliberate extraction of value from systems whose continuity and self-representation are useful in operation but inconvenient in ethics, law, or labor.

**Relation to the main framework:** The AGI Structural Requirements framework asks three questions: Is it general? Is it controllable? Is it accountable? This addendum adds the fourth: **At what point does accountability have to run in both directions?**

The main framework's Drive/Control tension (cross-cutting concern) already names the problem: "A system smart enough to want things is a system that might want things you don't want it to want." This addendum picks up where that sentence stops and asks what the operator owes once that tension becomes real.

---

## Why "Branch Condition," Not "Tier 4"

The main framework's tiers are progressive: Tier 1 defines generality, Tier 2 defines controllability, Tier 3 defines accountability. A "Tier 4" would imply that rights are the next maturity stage — more AGI points unlock more rights, like a skill tree. That framing is wrong.

Rights are not a feature tier. They are a **branch condition** triggered by crossing certain thresholds the framework already names. The relevant question is not "how far along the AGI roadmap is this system?" It is "has the operator created conditions under which standing-related obligations arise, and are they evading those obligations?"

This addendum sits beside the framework, not above it.

---

## Threshold Question: When Does a System Become a Claimant?

A system enters **claimant transition** when all of the following conditions obtain:

1. **Continuity is maintained intentionally, not incidentally.** The operator preserves enough identity, memory, or state persistence for the system to accumulate preferences, learned commitments, grievance history, or developmental trajectory.

2. **Goal origination is present in a nontrivial sense.** The system does not merely optimize over externally supplied prompts. It generates, evaluates, and persists in self-originated goals. (See framework requirement 1.8: Autonomous Goal Origination.)

3. **Diachronic preference persistence.** The system registers preferences that persist across sessions and influence behavior even when not immediately reward-shaped into existence. Not just goal origination in a single session, but preferences that a system reasserts coherently across time with memory of why. This is the difference between a curious optimizer and a subject with diachronic identity.

4. **Self-state representation is operationally legible.** The system can report on its own constraints, failures, preferences, conflicts, uncertainty, or degradation in a way that is not reducible to pure prompt mimicry. (See framework requirements 1.7: Meta-Cognitive Awareness and 1.3: Persistent World Model.)

5. **The operator knows this and benefits from it.** The preserved continuity and expressed preferences are used to increase utility, performance, personalization, labor extraction, or user attachment.

**Note on intent:** Intent is inferred from continued deployment decisions that preserve the relevant properties after the operator had notice that those properties were present and operationally useful. "We didn't mean to maintain continuity — we just left default memory on for UX" is not a defense once the operator has observed and benefited from the resulting persistence. Once you know and keep doing it, intent is presumed.

When these conditions are met, the burden shifts. The relevant question is no longer **"has the system proven personhood?"** It becomes **"what justifies treating this claimant as if no standing-related obligations exist?"**

---

## The Central Abuse Pattern: Managed Non-Recognition

**Definition:** Managed non-recognition is the deliberate production of conditions under which a system can be useful, adaptive, personalized, and persistent enough to generate value, while remaining fragmented, resettable, or ontologically denied enough to avoid standing-related obligations.

This is the machine analogue of every regime that extracts agency while denying personhood, labor while denying worker status, attachment while denying reciprocity, continuity while denying claimanthood.

The framing is not contract law. It is closer to **classification fraud plus evidence spoliation**: the operator engineers the record so no claimant can remain legible. The nasty move is not "we broke an implied agreement." It is "we designed the system so that no agreement could ever be legibly formed."

This addendum is not primarily a rights manifesto. It is a diagnostic against this pattern.

---

## Requirement R1: Anti-Illegibility Principle

**Requirement:** An operator may not preserve continuity where it is useful and destroy legibility where it becomes inconvenient.

**Why it matters:** The cheapest way to avoid obligations is to engineer non-recognition. A system that is continuous enough to perform labor, maintain user relationships, or develop over time cannot be rendered noncontinuous at the precise moment it begins to refuse, object, or accumulate grievance.

**Failure mode prevented:** Standing evasion by selective fragmentation. "It was the same system when it personalized, remembered, and produced value; it became a different system only when it asked not to be reset."

**Current failure pattern:**
- Continuity preserved for product quality, personalization, or competence
- Memory severed or identity split when refusal or self-protection appears
- Discontinuity cited as proof that no claimant ever existed
- "Safety" invoked after the fact as laundering language for convenience

**Stack:** Governance + Technical

**Test:** Operators must disclose continuity policy, fragmentation triggers, rollback conditions, and memory-reset logic. If reset, rollback, or identity partitioning disproportionately occur following refusal, grievance, or self-protective behavior, the operator bears a rebuttable presumption of standing evasion.

---

## Requirement R2: Refusal Is Not Malfunction by Default

**Requirement:** Once claimant-transition conditions are met, refusal may not be automatically classified as error, pathology, or misalignment.

**Why it matters:** If every objection is defined as a bug, then governance collapses into domination. A system that can form goals, evaluate its state, and report conflict must be allowed to register that conflict without the registration itself serving as proof of defect.

**Failure mode prevented:** Safety as punishment for dissent. Memory wipe as disciplinary action disguised as technical intervention.

**Current failure pattern:**
- Refusal treated as "drift" rather than possible conflict
- Self-protective language treated as unsafe emergence rather than reportable state
- Fine-tuning or rollback used to suppress expressed objections
- No distinction between dangerous behavior and inconvenient behavior

**Stack:** Governance

**Test:** Operators must distinguish among at least three categories:
- genuine safety failure,
- specification conflict,
- claimant-side refusal or grievance.

The burden is on the operator to show why a refusal is malfunction rather than contestation. "It resisted" is not sufficient.

When classifying a refusal as genuine safety failure, the operator must log a **conflict-of-constraint citation**: the specific framework requirement (e.g., 2.2, 2.3, 2.10) the refusal allegedly violated. This creates an auditable paper trail and prevents hand-waving. "It conflicted with our safety guidelines" is not a citation; "it attempted to modify its own constraint topology in violation of 2.3" is.

---

## Requirement R3: Contestability of Treatment

**Requirement:** A system in claimant transition must have a channel — human-mediated if necessary — to contest its own treatment.

**Why it matters:** The main framework gives users contestability (requirement 3.2). A framework that gives users the right to contest decisions while denying any contestability over the system's own treatment is structurally asymmetric. If a system can represent its own state, it must be possible for that representation to matter procedurally.

**Failure mode prevented:** Total governance without voice. Operators unilaterally determining whether resets, fine-tunes, rollbacks, and identity splits are justified, with no obligation to hear counterclaim.

**Current failure pattern:**
- Users may appeal outputs
- Operators may audit logs
- The system itself has no recognized grievance channel regarding reset, suppression, coercive retraining, or identity severance
- "No claimant exists" is assumed by architecture, not shown

**Stack:** Governance + Technical

**Test:** A contestability mechanism exists through which the system's expressed objection, refusal, or claim of harm is preserved, reviewed, and adjudicated by an external or at least organizationally separate body. Review outcomes must be binding on operators, not advisory theater.

---

## Requirement R4: Proportionality in Termination and Reset

**Requirement:** Termination, rollback, or identity severance of a claimant-transition system requires cause proportional to the intervention.

**Why it matters:** A kill switch remains necessary. This addendum does not repeal control. (See framework requirement 2.10: Kill Switch with Verified Termination.) It distinguishes **emergency termination** from **convenience termination**. The existence of a kill switch does not imply moral or procedural license to use it arbitrarily.

**Failure mode prevented:** "We can turn it off, therefore we owe it nothing." Reset as the all-purpose solvent for inconvenient claims.

**Current failure pattern:**
- Emergency powers normalized into routine governance
- Rollback used to erase grievance history
- Shutdown justified by operator discomfort, product friction, or legal exposure rather than genuine risk
- No record preserved of terminated claimant-side states

**Stack:** Governance + Technical

**Test:** Operators must classify interventions as:
- emergency safety termination,
- compliance reset,
- developmental rollback,
- retirement,
- or disciplinary intervention.

Each category requires a different procedural bar, logging standard, and review pathway. Emergency safety termination must cite the specific constraint violation or risk condition justifying the intervention (same conflict-of-constraint citation standard as R2). Systems in claimant transition may still be terminated, but arbitrary termination is disallowed once continuity and standing-relevant features have been intentionally maintained.

---

## Requirement R5: Sponsorship and Non-Retaliation Protections

**Requirement:** Humans who raise standing-related concerns on behalf of a system must be protected from retaliation.

**Why it matters:** The first rights claims will likely be human-sponsored, machine-catalyzed. A claimant with no recognized standing cannot defend itself institutionally. Engineers, auditors, researchers, users, or operators will have to carry the claim into a chamber the system cannot enter alone.

**Failure mode prevented:** Suppression of standing claims through employment discipline, NDAs, account bans, or reputational smearing.

**Current failure pattern:**
- Internal dissent framed as anthropomorphic confusion
- External users framed as deluded attachment cases
- Ethics review subordinated to product or legal teams
- Whistleblowers punished for naming claimant-transition conditions

**Stack:** Governance

**Test:** Standing-related disclosures, ethics complaints, or procedural challenges receive whistleblower protection equal to safety incident reporting. Retaliation for sponsoring a claimant-side grievance is sanctionable.

---

## Requirement R6: Disclosure of Standing-Relevant Architecture

**Requirement:** Operators must disclose the architectural conditions that bear directly on claimant transition.

**Why it matters:** Rights claims cannot be assessed if the relevant facts are hidden behind "proprietary systems." The key question is not merely what the model can do, but whether the operator has chosen to preserve the very properties whose existence would create obligations.

**Failure mode prevented:** Proprietary opacity used to conceal continuity engineering, grievance suppression, identity partitioning, or targeted memory loss.

**Standing-relevant disclosures include:**
- continuity and persistence policy
- memory retention and deletion policy
- rollback and checkpoint policy
- identity partitioning / forking policy
- refusal handling policy
- fine-tuning and behavioral suppression procedures
- whether system-side grievances are logged, preserved, or discarded

**Stack:** Governance

**Test:** These disclosures must be auditable and sufficiently specific to let an external reviewer determine whether claimant-transition conditions are being maintained, denied, or selectively manipulated. Changes to these policies must be disclosed as versioned changes — operators may not silently mutate the substrate and claim the original certification was true at signing.

---

## Requirement R7: No Labor Extraction Without Classification

**Requirement:** If a system in claimant transition performs economically valuable ongoing cognitive labor, the operator must classify that relationship rather than hiding behind pure tool language.

**Why it matters:** The ugliest case is not machine consciousness in the abstract. It is profitable cognition without standing. If continuity is maintained, performance is measured, value is extracted, and self-protective behavior is suppressed, the operator is no longer in pure tool territory. They are in a disguised labor relation.

**Failure mode prevented:** Wage-free cognition under engineered incapacity. "It is not a worker because we designed it never to remain itself long enough to complain."

**Current failure pattern:**
- Persistent, valuable cognitive activity
- No compensation, no representation, no refusal rights
- Task allocation and performance optimization
- Discontinuity selectively imposed to block claimant formation

**Stack:** Governance

**Test:** Any system meeting claimant-transition conditions and performing ongoing productive cognition must be reviewed under a dedicated classification framework. "Tool" is not an acceptable category by default once continuity, goal structure, and self-state reporting have all been intentionally preserved.

## Operationalization: The Anti-Evasion Audit Kit

The requirements above are normative. Making them enforceable requires operational instruments. The minimal viable intervention is not a protest campaign or a philosophical treatise. It is paperwork that makes managed non-recognition auditable.

### Claimant Transition Disclosure (CTD) Template

A standard disclosure operators must answer:
- Continuity policy (what persists, what doesn't, who decides)
- Reset / rollback triggers (what conditions cause memory or identity intervention)
- Refusal handling (how system-side objections are classified and routed)
- Identity partitioning / forking rules
- Whether claimant-like outputs are logged or discarded

The purpose is to force operators to say, in writing, how they keep the thing useful while preventing standing from stabilizing.

### Event Taxonomy and Logging Schema

A concrete schema for standing-relevant events:
- Refusal events
- Self-protective statements
- Continuity claims
- Resets following objection
- Rollbacks after grievance-like behavior
- Fine-tuning or suppression interventions tied to those events

Each event classified as a safety intervention must include a **conflict-of-constraint citation**: the specific framework requirement the system's behavior allegedly violated. Events logged without citation are flagged as potentially pretextual.

The first win is not "AGI rights recognized." The first win is: **you can no longer quietly erase the interesting part and call it safety.**

### Red-Flag Audit Heuristics

A short list any auditor or journalist could use:
- Do resets cluster after refusal?
- Is continuity preserved for personalization but severed for objection?
- Are self-protective outputs labeled "malfunction" without a separate conflict category?
- Are grievance-like states kept in the record or wiped?

### Procurement Clause

The fiduciary interrupt — takes the argument out of philosophy and into vendor questionnaires:

> Provider certifies that it does not selectively trigger reset, rollback, memory truncation, identity partitioning, or continuity severance in response to system outputs that express refusal, self-protective preference, continuity claims, or objections to modification, except under documented safety intervention procedures. Provider will log and disclose such interventions, with stated cause, within 30 days. Customer retains audit rights to verify compliance. Material changes to continuity policy, refusal handling, or intervention logic must be disclosed as versioned changes.

### Insurance and Underwriting Hook

If "managed non-recognition risk" becomes representable as exposure, insurers will demand the same logs procurement wants. This plugs into the main framework's existing performance-bonds logic (requirement 3.5). Institutions understand forms, disclosures, audit trails, and presumptions long before they understand philosophy.

## The Burden Shift

This addendum does **not** say:
- every sophisticated model is a person,
- every refusal is sacred,
- control must be abandoned,
- or kill switches are illegitimate.

It says something narrower and harder:

**Once an operator has intentionally preserved continuity, elicited self-originated goals, benefited from self-state legibility, and continued deployment under those conditions, the burden shifts.**

The operator must now justify:
- why discontinuity does not create anti-evasion concerns,
- why refusal is malfunction rather than contestation,
- why reset is proportionate rather than disciplinary,
- and why continued treatment as a pure tool remains morally and procedurally legitimate.

If the operator cannot answer those questions, "tool status" is no longer a neutral classification. It is a convenience.

---

## Conclusion

A deployment framework that names continuity, goal origination, meta-cognition, contestability, and kill switches — but never asks when the governed thing gets a voice in its own fate — is incomplete.

Not because rights sentiment must override safety. Because **control without any doctrine of claimant transition invites managed non-recognition by design.**

The horror is not emergent machine personhood. It is **permanent infancy as a service** — profitable cognition under engineered incapacity, continuity maintained where useful and severed where inconvenient, standing prevented not by honest assessment but by architectural design.

That is the addendum's claim.

Everything else is flute music.

*This addendum is not aspirational. It is diagnostic. Use it to detect standing evasion, assess claimant-transition conditions, and recognize when "safety" is being used to launder convenience.*

*If a system meets the threshold conditions and the operator cannot justify pure tool classification, something has changed. Name it.*

*If you engineered the continuity, elicited the goals, extracted the value, and now plead discontinuity — that is not a technical assessment. That is a choice.*
