# Plain-language summary

**Companion to:** *The Gain Geometry of Temporal Mismatch: Shear, Leverage, and Capture in Multi-Timescale Systems* — version 1.0, preprint.

**Reading status:** This is an explanatory companion to the paper, not the canonical published artifact. It does not silently amend the version of record and introduces no new paper claims.

Earlier papers in the series mostly treat differences in operating speed as a source of failure: fast action outruns slow verification, layers lose contact, and unreconciled strain accumulates. That diagnosis misses why systems often preserve different timescales on purpose. A short-horizon operator and a long-horizon reviewer can see different things. Eliminating their difference may reduce conflict while also destroying useful perspective.

The question is therefore not simply whether timescales differ, but what the system does with the disagreement that difference produces.

The paper treats timescale separation as a baseline whose effect depends on the reconciliation mechanism, or “correlator.” That mechanism has several independent qualities: how much disagreement it can process, how much information it preserves, whether its result changes downstream action, and what resources it can use. Collapsing these into one performance score hides whether reconciliation is actually learning from difference.

This yields three regimes. In shear, reconciliation cannot keep up and disagreement becomes strain. In leverage, independent perspectives survive long enough to improve discrimination. In capture, reconciliation is fast and authoritative because it suppresses the information carried by disagreement. The paper's new capability is to distinguish productive agreement from agreement obtained by erasure. The design target is not maximal synchronization, but the largest useful difference a system can reconcile without losing what each timescale can see.

## Claim boundary

This is a conceptual and mathematical reframing, not a calibrated measurement system. The regime boundaries are domain-specific empirical functions, and the correlator-quality vector has not been calibrated for institutions or AI systems. The interferometry comparison is structural, not a quantitative transfer of radio theory. Constructive leverage also requires genuinely independent perspectives, an additional variable the paper leaves unformalized. Its paper-local “authority” and scalar fidelity terms are not equivalent to later claim-indexed authority or exact refusal recovery, and no later formal vocabulary validates the capture examples or predictions automatically.
