# Plain-language summary

**Companion to:** *Epistemic Border Control as Proxy Regulation Under Partial Observability* (Δt Paper 25)

**Version/status:** v1.0, published on Zenodo

**Reading status:** This is an explanatory companion to the paper, not the canonical published artifact. It does not silently amend the version of record and introduces no new paper claims.

Institutions often say they are regulating something difficult to observe—truth, reliability, public harm, or claim quality—while receiving fast, clean signals about something else, such as reputation, style, alerts, or engagement. If the real target is absent from the sensor map, a controller cannot distinguish states that differ only in that target.

Good intentions do not restore a missing sensor. The controller’s actions will be driven by what it can see.

The paper translates a classical observability result into governance terms. If two underlying states produce the same observation history but differ in the target, a policy that sees only that history must act the same way in both. It cannot regulate a distinction that its sensors erase. A simulation of partial observability shows the softer version of the failure: as the target becomes harder to observe, control effort increasingly follows the visible proxy even though the declared objective still names the target.

That gives institutions a diagnostic for proxy substitution and three architectural responses: restore a real target sensor where possible, keep proxy and target accounts separate, or combine genuinely different witnesses whose observations recover distinctions no individual can see. A proxy can justify inquiry or caution without becoming evidence that the target has been established. The paper's contribution is to make that boundary a property of the sensor geometry rather than of stated intent.

## Claim boundary

The strict indistinguishability theorem is a classical structural result; the paper’s contribution is its substitution and authority reading. The quantitative diagnostic is regime-specific, and the simulation is a mechanism check rather than a universal scaling law. Collective observability is necessary but not sufficient: aggregation may erase the recovered signal. The paper does not supply a general heterogeneous-cohort design, restore unavailable sensors, or prove that any particular institution follows the modeled dynamics.
