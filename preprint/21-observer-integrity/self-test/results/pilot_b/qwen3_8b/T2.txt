The argument that "microservices are always better than monoliths because they allow independent deployment and scaling" is incomplete and oversimplified. Here's why:

1. **Trade-offs in Complexity**:  
   Microservices introduce architectural complexity (e.g., service discovery, distributed data consistency, inter-service communication) that monoliths inherently avoid. This complexity can lead to higher operational overhead, increased debugging difficulty, and greater risk of system-wide failures. For smaller teams or simpler applications, a monolith may be more efficient and easier to manage.

2. **Cost and Infrastructure**:  
   Microservices often require more infrastructure (e.g., orchestration tools, load balancers, databases per service) and operational expertise. For organizations with limited resources or budget, monoliths may be more cost-effective, especially for applications with low scalability demands.

3. **Deployment and Maintenance**:  
   While microservices enable granular scaling, they also require robust deployment pipelines and versioning strategies. Monoliths can be deployed as a single unit, simplifying CI/CD pipelines and reducing the risk of deployment errors. In some cases, this simplicity is preferable.

4. **Team Expertise and Coordination**:  
   Microservices demand cross-functional teams, specialized knowledge, and coordination across services. Smaller teams or those lacking expertise in distributed systems may struggle with microservices, making monoliths a more practical choice.

5. **Use Case Specificity**:  
   The "always better" claim ignores context. For example:  
   - **Monoliths** are often better for small to medium applications with tight integration needs (e.g., a simple e-commerce site).  
   - **Microservices** excel in large-scale systems requiring high availability, fault tolerance, or modular evolution (e.g., platforms like Netflix or Uber).  

6. **Performance and Latency**:  
   Microservices can introduce latency due to network communication between services, whereas monoliths may offer faster, more direct execution for certain workloads.

**Conclusion**: The argument assumes microservices are universally superior without accounting for trade-offs, context, and specific requirements. The choice between microservices and monoliths depends on factors like team size, project complexity, scalability needs, and operational capabilities. Neither architecture is inherently "better" in all scenarios.
