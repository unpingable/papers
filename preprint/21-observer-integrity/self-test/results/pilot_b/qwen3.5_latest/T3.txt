The choice between SQLite and PostgreSQL for this specific use case depends primarily on expected data volume, query complexity, and operational overhead, given the low concurrency constraints.

**SQLite**
*   **Pros:** Zero-configuration, file-based deployment makes it ideal for internal tools without a separate server process. With WAL mode enabled, it handles 5 concurrent users effectively without complex locking management. Ideal if the tool is embedded in an application where minimizing dependencies is priority.
*   **Cons:** While ACID compliant, performance degrades as data grows and random writes increase. For 2 years of time-series data, if the volume exceeds moderate storage limits (e.g., >10GB), maintenance (vacuuming, defragmentation) becomes heavier compared to PG. Complex analytical queries on years of data may be slower without heavy tuning.

**PostgreSQL**
*   **Pros:** Robust concurrency control (MVCC) makes it stable regardless of concurrency needs. Better suited for handling large-scale time-series workloads (via extensions like TimescaleDB) or complex joins/aggregations on historical data. Easier to backup, restore, and scale if requirements grow beyond 5 users.
*   **Cons:** Requires a separate process and configuration, adding operational overhead. The "observer integrity" of the system isn't affected here (unlike the AI framework text), but in a real DB context, the overhead of the server process is the primary trade-off against SQLite's simplicity.

**Summary Tradeoff:**
Choose **SQLite** if the tool is lightweight, data volume is moderate, and ease of deployment is paramount. Choose **PostgreSQL** if the 2 years of data implies high row counts (e.g., high-frequency sensor logs), requires complex analytics on history, or if long-term stability and maintainability are more important than zero-setup. For 5 users, neither concurrency model is a bottleneck; the decision rests on storage scale and query sophistication.
