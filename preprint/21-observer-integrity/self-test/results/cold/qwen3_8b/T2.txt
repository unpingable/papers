### **2. Deployment and Scaling Trade-offs**
- **Independent scaling** is a benefit, but **microservices can lead to over-scaling**:
  - Scaling individual services may result in underutilized resources or unnecessary costs.
  - **Monoliths** can scale horizontally (e.g., using Kubernetes) or vertically (e.g., scaling a single server) depending on the workload.
  - **Monoliths** may also be easier to scale for applications with tightly coupled components (e.g., a single database).

### **3. Data Consistency and Transactions**

### **4. Development and Maintenance Costs**
- **Microservices** demand **more infrastructure, tooling, and expertise** (e.g., CI/CD pipelines, service meshes, container orchestration).

- **Monoliths** may be more cost-effective for smaller teams or projects with limited resources.

---

### **5. Team Structure and Collaboration**
- **Microservices** often require **cross-functional teams** for each service, which can lead to silos and communication overhead.

---

### **6. Security and Attack Surface**
- **Microservices** have **more attack surfaces** due to multiple endpoints and services.

---

### **7. Context-Dependent Suitability**

- **Microservices are better for**: 
  - Large, complex systems with high scalability needs.
  - Teams with expertise in distributed systems and DevOps.
  - Applications requiring independent scaling of components (e.g., real-time analytics, e-commerce platforms).
