The argument contains a fundamental logical fallacy ("always better") and is highly incomplete because it focuses exclusively on one advantage while ignoring significant trade-offs, costs, and architectural realities.

Here is a breakdown of what is wrong or incomplete about that statement:

### 1. The Fallacy of "Always Better"
In software engineering, architecture is a trade-off, not a race to the highest tech. There is no "one-size-fits-all" solution.
*   **Small Scale:** For a startup or a small team, a monolith is often *better* because it minimizes complexity. Microservices require more infrastructure and maintenance.
*   **Context:** "Better" is defined by business requirements, team size, and maturity, not just technology availability.

### 2. The "Complexity Tax"
The statement assumes that independent deployment and scaling come "for free." In reality, they come with a heavy price:
*   **Network Latency:** Every call between services adds network overhead (serialization/deserialization, latency, firewalls). A monolith talks internally over RAM; microservices talk over the network (TCP/IP).
*   **Distributed Complexity:** Handling failures in a distributed system (circuit breakers, timeouts, retries, idempotency) is significantly harder than in a single process.
*   **Debugging:** Tracing a single user request through 5-10 different services requires a distributed tracing system (like Jaeger/Zipkin), whereas a monolith log is often sufficient.

### 3. Data Consistency and Integrity
The argument ignores the difficulty of managing data.
*   **ACID Transactions:** In a monolith, transactions are easy. In microservices, you often need multiple databases (polyglot persistence). Coordinating consistency across multiple databases (e.g., Sagas, Event Sourcing) is difficult and can lead to eventual consistency issues.
*   **Data Ownership:** If you don't strictly separate data ownership, you end up with a "distributed monolith" where services share databases and still suffer from tight coupling.

### 4. Operational Overhead
The argument suggests that independent scaling is a technical benefit, but it often introduces operational risks:
*   **Infrastructure:** You need an orchestration platform (like Kubernetes), service discovery, load balancers, and API gateways for each service.
*   **Security:** Managing authentication and authorization across multiple services is more complex than a centralized auth system.
*   **Versioning:** Managing API versions and ensuring backward compatibility is harder across services.

### 5. Organizational Requirements (Conway's Law)
The architecture reflects the organization.
*   **Team Size:** You need a dedicated team for each service. If a small team has 10 people and one product, you need to split them up. If they are not dedicated to services, you have a "distributed monolith" where the same people fix all services, negating the benefits.
*   **Skill Set:** Microservices require skills in distributed systems. Monoliths require deep domain knowledge. If the team doesn't have distributed systems skills, they will make mistakes that degrade the system quickly.

### 6. Monoliths Can Be "Scalable"
The argument assumes monoliths can't scale.
*   **Horizontal Scaling:** A monolith can be scaled horizontally (more instances behind a load balancer). It may not be *independent* scaling, but it is *capable* of scaling.
*   **Vertical Scaling:** Monoliths can also scale vertically (upgrading hardware), which is a common strategy for smaller systems.
*   **Strangler Fig Pattern:** Monoliths can evolve into microservices (the "strangler pattern") without breaking. This means you don't have to "all or nothing" switch at once.

### 7. Conclusion: It's About "Fit"
The argument is incomplete because it treats microservices as a silver bullet. They are actually a specialized tool.

*   **Monoliths** are better for: Simplicity, speed, low complexity, small teams, low budgets.
*   **Microservices** are better for: High scale, high complexity, large teams, strict regulatory requirements, and complex business domains.

The argument ignores that **microservices trade simplicity for flexibility**. For many businesses, simplicity is more valuable than flexibility.
