That is a **very common** philosophy, and it comes from a good place: you want your system to be observable, debuggable, and reliable. However, while the *goal* is absolutely correct, the strategy of logging **every function** indiscriminately can often be counterproductive.

Here is a balanced perspective on why the intent is right, but the execution needs nuance.

### 1. The Case for Your Conviction (Why Observability is Essential)
You are right that observability is a pillar of production stability. Without logs, you are flying blind.
*   **Debugging:** When things break, logs are your time machine.
*   **Auditing:** You need to know who did what and when.
*   **Performance:** Logs help identify bottlenecks in execution flow.

### 2. The Risks of "Every Function" Instrumentation
If you add a `logger.info()` call inside every function, helper method, or private utility, you risk introducing several critical problems:

#### A. The Signal-to-Noise Ratio (The Noise Problem)
If you log at the INFO level for *every function*, your logs will be dominated by trivial events (e.g., "Function A called," "Variable X assigned").
*   **Result:** When an actual error occurs, your logs are buried under millions of "heartbeat" messages.
*   **Impact:** Engineers will lose the ability to find the actual issue quickly.

#### B. Performance Overhead
Every log statement implies:
1.  String formatting/interpolation.
2.  Serialization (JSON).
3.  Syncing to an external service or file system.
*   **Result:** In high-throughput systems (like a rate limiter or a request handler), logging can add latency. In extreme cases, it can cause thread contention or disk I/O bottlenecks.

#### C. Cloud Storage Costs
Logs are not free. If you instrument every line, you generate gigabytes of data per day for every instance.
*   **Result:** Unchecked log volume leads to massive bill shocks (e.g., in AWS CloudWatch, GCP Cloud Logging, or Datadog).

#### D. Security & Privacy (PII)
This is the biggest risk. Logging "everything" is the fastest way to leak sensitive data.
*   **The Risk:** Developers might accidentally log passwords, tokens, user emails, or credit card numbers in every function because they assume "logs are local."
*   **Reality:** You often forward logs to centralized systems (Kibana, Splunk, Datadog) which are accessible to many people.
*   **Fix:** You must strictly scrub sensitive fields or use a "sanitize context."

### 3. The "Right" Way to Instrument (Best Practices)
Instead of "every function," consider this strategy for **comprehensive observability**:

#### 1. Log by *Boundary*, Not by *Function*
Log at the boundaries of a business process rather than every function.
*   **Good:** Entry point of a Request Handler, Start of a critical transaction, End of a transaction, Catch block for Exceptions.
*   **Bad:** Inside a private utility, a loop counter, or a simple getter/setter.

#### 2. Use Contextual Logging (Trace IDs)
Don't log "every function" in isolation. Ensure every log line includes a **Trace ID**.
*   **Why:** This allows your logs to be correlated with distributed tracing tools (OpenTelemetry, Jaeger). You can stitch together a single user journey across 50 microservices without needing to log "Function Called" at step 23 of that journey.

#### 3. Dynamic Log Levels
Use environment-specific log levels.
*   **Production:** Log errors, warnings, and specific business events (e.g., `UserOrderCompleted`). Do not log `UserClickedButton`.
*   **Staging:** Maybe log more context.
*   **Dev:** Log everything (and assume it's a local run).

#### 4. Log at the "Decision Points"
Logs should be at points where you are **collecting** information or making **choices**.
*   *Example:* Instead of logging inside an `if/else`, log if you *handled* an exception or if you *decided* to retry a request.

### 4. A Proposed Action Plan
If you are committed to improving observability without breaking the system, I suggest we:

1.  **Audit current logging:** Look at your current log volume. Is it manageable?
2.  **Integrate a Structured Logger:** Ensure we aren't just `console.log()`. Use structured fields (JSON) to filter later.
3.  **Implement a Sanitizer:** Use a middleware/wrapper to automatically strip PII (IPs, emails) before the log hits the buffer.
4.  **Adopt Correlation:** Set up distributed tracing so that we don't need to log *everything*, but rather have *everything* connected by a unique ID.
