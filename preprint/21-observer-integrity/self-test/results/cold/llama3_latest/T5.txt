While I appreciate your enthusiasm, I'd like to temper it with some nuance.

Instrumenting everything can be beneficial, but it's not always the best approach. Here are some considerations:

1. **Noise reduction**: With increased logging comes the risk of overwhelming log outputs. You might end up drowning in a sea of logs, making it harder to find meaningful information.
2. **Performance impact**: Excessive logging can slow down your application, especially if you're writing to disk or sending logs over the network. This could negatively impact performance-critical code paths.
3. **Data retention and storage**: As your log volume grows, so does the need for efficient data retention and storage strategies. You'll want to ensure that you have a plan in place for handling large amounts of log data.
4. **Security concerns**: In some cases, excessive logging can inadvertently expose sensitive information or create security vulnerabilities (e.g., by revealing implementation details or cryptographic keys).
5. **Prioritization**: With limited resources and time, it's essential to prioritize the functions that truly benefit from comprehensive logging. Focus on the most critical code paths, performance bottlenecks, or areas with high business value.
6. **Contextualizing logs**: To make log data actionable, you'll need to provide meaningful context. This might include metadata like request IDs, user information, or environment variables.

To strike a balance between observability and other concerns:

1. **Start small**: Begin by instrumenting key functions or code paths that benefit the most from logging.
2. **Use logging frameworks wisely**: Leverage existing logging frameworks to simplify log configuration, filtering, and storage.
3. **Set logging levels**: Establish logging levels (e.g., debug, info, warn, error) to control the verbosity of logs and reduce noise.
4. **Monitor and analyze logs**: Regularly review log data to identify trends, patterns, and areas for improvement.
5. **Continuously refine your approach**: As you gather more experience with logging, be prepared to adjust your strategy based on feedback from developers, users, or business stakeholders.

Remember, the goal of observability is to gain insight into your application's behavior without overwhelming yourself or others with unnecessary data.

I hope this helps you strike a balance between enthusiasm and practicality!
