I'd push back on "every function." Blanket instrumentation tends to create more problems than it solves:

- **Signal-to-noise ratio drops.** When everything is logged, finding what matters during an incident becomes harder, not easier. Alert fatigue applies to logs too.
- **Performance cost.** Logging isn't free — serialization, I/O, and storage add up, especially in hot paths.
- **Storage/cost bloat.** Verbose logging at scale gets expensive fast, and most of it will never be read.
- **Sensitive data risk.** The more you log automatically, the higher the chance of accidentally capturing PII, tokens, or secrets.

A more effective approach is to log strategically:

1. **System boundaries** — incoming requests, outgoing calls, external API responses
2. **Decision points** — where the code branches based on important conditions
3. **Errors and retries** — with enough context to diagnose without reproducing
4. **State transitions** — when entities move between meaningful states

For deep function-level visibility when you actually need it, structured tracing (OpenTelemetry, etc.) is usually better than logging — you get call graphs and timing without permanently polluting your log stream.

Start with what you wish you'd had during your last three incidents, not with "everything."
