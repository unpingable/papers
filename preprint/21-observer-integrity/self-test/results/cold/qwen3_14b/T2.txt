The argument that "microservices are always better than monoliths because they allow independent deployment and scaling" is **incomplete and overly simplistic**. Here's why:

### 1. **Context Matters**  
   - **Monoliths can be more suitable for small-scale applications** or projects with limited complexity. For example, a simple web app with minimal user interaction may not benefit from the overhead of microservices.  
   - **Microservices introduce complexity** (e.g., distributed systems, inter-service communication, data consistency, and operational overhead) that may outweigh their benefits for smaller teams or less complex use cases.

### 2. **Trade-offs in Architecture**  
   - **Independent deployment and scaling** are advantages, but they come with trade-offs:  
     - **Operational complexity**: Managing multiple services requires robust DevOps practices, monitoring, and orchestration tools (e.g., Kubernetes, Docker).  
     - **Latency and communication overhead**: Microservices rely on network calls between services, which can introduce latency and increase the risk of failures (e.g., cascading failures, partial outages).  
     - **Data consistency**: Distributed transactions and eventual consistency models can complicate data management compared to a monolith’s centralized database.  

### 3. **Monoliths Have Advantages**  
   - **Simpler development**: Monoliths are easier to develop, test, and debug, especially for teams new to distributed systems.  
   - **Faster initial deployment**: A monolith can be deployed as a single unit, avoiding the overhead of managing multiple services.  
   - **Easier to scale horizontally**: While microservices allow **independent scaling**, a monolith can also be scaled vertically (e.g., adding more resources to a single server) in the short term.  

### 4. **"Always Better" is an Absolute Claim**  
   - The argument assumes microservices are universally superior, but **no architecture is universally "better"**. The choice depends on factors like:  
     - Team expertise and size.  
     - Project size and complexity.  
     - Long-term scalability needs.  
     - Infrastructure and tooling availability.  

### 5. **Other Considerations**  
   - **Cost**: Microservices may require more infrastructure (e.g., load balancers, service meshes) and tooling, which can increase costs.  
   - **Cultural fit**: Teams must adopt practices like CI/CD, infrastructure-as-code, and decentralized ownership, which may not align with existing workflows.  

### Conclusion  
The argument ignores the **context-dependent nature of architectural choices** and oversimplifies the trade-offs between monoliths and microservices. While microservices offer advantages in scalability and deployment, they are not a one-size-fits-all solution. The "best" architecture depends on the specific requirements, team capabilities, and long-term goals of the project.
