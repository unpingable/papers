**Key Tradeoffs Between SQLite and PostgreSQL for the Use Case:**

1. **Concurrency & Scalability:**
   - **SQLite:** Limited by file-locking and lacks server processes, which may struggle with concurrent writes (even with 5 users) and scaling beyond simple use cases.
   - **PostgreSQL:** Designed for multi-user environments, supports concurrent connections, and handles scaling better for long-term data growth.

2. **Time-Series Data Handling:**
   - **SQLite:** No native support for time-series optimizations (e.g., partitioning, indexing). Performance may degrade with large datasets.
   - **PostgreSQL:** Offers advanced indexing (e.g., BRIN, GIN), partitioning, and extensions (e.g., TimescaleDB) for efficient time-series storage and querying.

3. **Data Volume & Long-Term Storage:**
   - **SQLite:** Limited by file size (theoretically ~14EB, but practical limits depend on OS and hardware). May become inefficient for 2+ years of data.
   - **PostgreSQL:** Better suited for large datasets, with robust backup/recovery and archival strategies.

4. **Feature Set & Complexity:**
   - **SQLite:** Lightweight, ACID-compliant, and easy to deploy. Lacks advanced features (e.g., stored procedures, full-text search).
   - **PostgreSQL:** Richer feature set (e.g., JSONB, replication, advanced analytics) but requires more setup and maintenance.

5. **Future-Proofing:**
   - **SQLite:** Easier to use now but may become a bottleneck if the tool evolves (e.g., more users, complex queries).
   - **PostgreSQL:** More future-proof for potential growth, even if current needs are minimal.

**Recommendation:**  
If the tool is strictly internal, low-concurrency, and unlikely to grow, **SQLite** may suffice for simplicity. However, for **reliable long-term storage of time-series data** and potential scalability, **PostgreSQL** is the better choice despite its higher complexity.
