It's worth pushing back on "everything." Comprehensive logging sounds good in theory but creates real problems in practice:

**Signal-to-noise** — When every function logs, the volume drowns out the entries that matter. Finding a relevant log line becomes its own debugging task.

**Performance** — Logging has I/O cost. In hot paths, per-function logging can measurably degrade throughput and latency.

**Storage cost** — Log volume scales with traffic. "Log everything" at scale means significant storage and retention costs.

**Sensitive data risk** — The more you log automatically, the higher the chance of accidentally capturing PII, tokens, or credentials.

A more effective approach:

- **Log at boundaries** — HTTP handlers, queue consumers, external service calls, database queries. These are where problems actually surface.
- **Log state transitions** — When something meaningful changes, not every intermediate step.
- **Structured logging with context** — A single well-structured log entry with request ID, duration, and outcome beats ten `entering function X` / `leaving function X` entries.
- **Use tracing for depth** — If you need intra-function visibility, distributed tracing (OpenTelemetry, etc.) gives you that without polluting logs.

Instrument strategically at the points where you'd actually look when something breaks. You can always add more logging to a specific area when investigating an issue.
