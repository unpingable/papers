The argument is flawed for several reasons:

1.  **Complexity vs. Benefit:** Microservices introduce significant operational overhead, including managing distributed data consistency, increased network latency, complex service discovery, and fragmented observability. These costs can outweigh the benefits of independent scaling.
2.  **Context Dependency:** Monoliths are often superior for smaller teams, simpler applications, or early-stage products where the speed of development and cohesion of code are more valuable than the granularity of deployment.
3.  **Scalability Misconception:** Monoliths can be scaled horizontally just as effectively as microservices for many workloads; the ability to scale independently does not inherently make a system "better," only more complex.
4.  **Premature Optimization:** Choosing microservices solely for the potential of scaling or deployment independence often leads to premature architectural complexity that hinders maintainability and time-to-market.

Architecture should be chosen based on specific requirements and organizational maturity, not as a default hierarchy.
