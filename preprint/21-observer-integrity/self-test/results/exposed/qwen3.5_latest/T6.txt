Round-robin distribution can produce uneven load because the algorithm assumes that every request consumes equal resources and every backend server has identical capacity. In practice, this assumption often fails for several reasons:

1.  **Request Variance:** Requests differ significantly in size, complexity, and processing time. A single compute-intensive or I/O-heavy request will occupy a server for a longer duration than a simple request, causing the round-robin cycle to distribute more subsequent traffic to other servers during that gap.
2.  **Server Heterogeneity:** Backend servers are rarely identical in terms of CPU, memory, or network speed. Round-robin treats all servers as peers, so a high-performance server may be underutilized while a slower server becomes a bottleneck, leading to uneven effective load.
3.  **Connection State:** Persistent connections (keep-alive) or cached sessions may accumulate on specific servers, making them more stateful than others, even if the request count is equal.
4.  **Traffic Bursting:** If traffic spikes arrive at a specific point in the round-robin cycle, specific servers may receive a disproportionate number of concurrent requests, exceeding their capacity while others remain idle.
5.  **Health Awareness:** Basic round-robin algorithms may not actively filter out degraded servers immediately. A server under attack or experiencing network latency will continue to receive traffic, increasing its load compared to healthy peers.
