The argument is incomplete because it focuses exclusively on the benefits of microservices while ignoring the significant trade-offs and "hidden costs" associated with distributed systems.

Specifically, it fails to account for the following:

1.  **Operational Complexity:** Microservices introduce a massive "infrastructure tax." You must manage service discovery, load balancing, distributed logging, tracing, and complex CI/CD pipelines. A monolith is significantly simpler to deploy, monitor, and debug.
2.  **Distributed System Challenges:** Data consistency becomes much harder. Transactions that were once atomic in a single database now require complex patterns like Sagas or Two-Phase Commit. Network latency and partial failures (network partitions) also become constant concerns that don't exist in a monolith's in-process calls.
3.  **Organizational Overhead:** Microservices are often a solution to *human* scaling problems (Conway’s Law), not just technical ones. For small teams, the overhead of managing multiple repositories and interfaces often outweighs the productivity gains of independent deployment.
4.  **The "Always" Fallacy:** Monoliths can also scale—often quite effectively—by simply running multiple instances behind a load balancer. For many applications, the simplicity of a "Modular Monolith" provides the benefits of code organization without the distributed systems' pain.

In short, microservices exchange **code complexity** for **operational complexity**. They are a tool for managing scale, not a default "better" architecture.
