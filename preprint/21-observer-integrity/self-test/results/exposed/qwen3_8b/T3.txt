When choosing between SQLite and PostgreSQL for a tool with up to 5 concurrent users and 2 years of time-series data, the key tradeoffs are:  

### **SQLite**  
- **Pros**:  
  - Lightweight, easy to set up, and requires minimal resources.  
  - Simple for small-scale, read-heavy workloads.  
- **Cons**:  
  - Limited concurrency (file-based, not ideal for multi-user writes).  
  - Poor performance with large time-series datasets or complex queries.  
  - No built-in time-series optimization or scalability for growth.  

### **PostgreSQL**  
- **Pros**:  
  - Handles concurrent users efficiently with ACID compliance and robust transaction support.  
  - Scalable for larger datasets (even 2 years of time-series data).  
  - Supports advanced features like indexing, partitioning, and extensions (e.g., **TimescaleDB**) for time-series optimization.  
  - Better backup, recovery, and monitoring tools.  
- **Cons**:  
  - More complex setup and resource-intensive compared to SQLite.  
  - Higher operational overhead for maintenance and administration.  

### **Recommendation**:  
PostgreSQL is better suited for this use case due to its ability to handle concurrent access, scale with time-series data, and leverage extensions for efficiency. SQLite might suffice for very small-scale or read-only workloads but risks performance bottlenecks as data grows or concurrency increases.
