# Plain-language summary

**Companion to:** *Receipt the Compiler: Propaganda as Hidden Epistemic Policy and the Architecture of Legible Memory* — version 1.0, preprint.

**Reading status:** This is an explanatory companion to the paper, not the canonical published artifact. It does not silently amend the version of record and introduces no new paper claims.

More evidence does not guarantee a more trustworthy public memory. An institution can retain every document while changing which documents are visible, how they are indexed, what weight they receive, which frames are attached, or which rule version turns them into action. Later evidence should revise earlier interpretations; the danger is an opaque process that rewrites why a past decision appears legitimate.

The paper models public memory as a graph that keeps events, evidence receipts, interpretive frames, policy rules, and institutional decisions separate. Its proposed epistemic governor does not prevent belief revision. Instead, it makes revisions legible: changes to frames, policies, classifications, and decisions produce typed, timestamped receipts; decisions cite the evidence and rule version used at the time; and “as-of” replay reconstructs what the system could have known before later evidence or policy arrived.

The central contribution is to move provenance up one layer. Keeping original documents is not enough when the rules that rank, frame, admit, or act on them can change invisibly. The paper shows how policy surgery, rationale backfill, hidden rule drift, and reordered histories can alter public memory without deleting a fact. A trustworthy system must therefore make both the record and the compiler that interprets it inspectable.

## Claim boundary

The governor produces auditable belief revision, not truth. Its Bayesian numbers are pedagogical, not calibrated estimates, and the paper presents an architecture and illustrative schemas rather than a deployed runtime. The type system can itself be captured; well-kept receipts can remain inaccessible; concentrated framing authority and slower governed processes remain risks.

Typing facts and decisions separately is necessary but does not by itself block rationale backfill. The operative protection also requires a stored native decision-time record, append-only origin/history-bound later events, and replay that cannot overwrite or recompute the earlier basis. Later stored-decision and break-glass results are narrow formal analogues; they do not prove the factor graph, posterior calculations, accessibility, or runtime schema.
