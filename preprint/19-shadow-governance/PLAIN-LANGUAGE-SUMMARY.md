# Plain-language summary

**Companion to:** *The System Beside the System: Shadow Governance and the Stabilization of Unauthorized Authority* (Δt Paper 19)

**Version/status:** v1.0.1, published on Zenodo

**Reading status:** This is an explanatory companion to the paper, not the canonical published artifact. It does not silently amend the version of record and introduces no new paper claims.

Formal rules are not always the rules that actually govern a system. A temporary exception may be faster than the approved process, become routine, acquire downstream dependencies, and eventually be taught as “how things work.” The written policy remains in place, but actual decisions follow an unofficial path that was never properly authorized.

At that point, simply enforcing the written rule may break the system. The workaround has become load-bearing.

The paper calls this condition **shadow governance**: state that exercises durable governing force without a valid promotion history. It explains stabilization through three forces—the workaround's practical advantage, the dependencies built around it, and fading memory of why the formal rule existed. When the formal layer remains visible while the unofficial layer performs the real control, the formal system has become a **Potemkin layer**.

This changes both detection and repair. Auditors have to compare documented authority with observed behavior and provenance, not merely confirm that policies exist. Once shadow rules are load-bearing, remediation is a migration problem: find and classify the shadow state, map its dependencies, then legitimize, quarantine, migrate, or replace it. The paper gives readers a way to distinguish an isolated exception from an unofficial governance regime that the system now depends on.

## Claim boundary

The divergence index is diagnostic rather than a calibrated universal metric, and the worked examples are illustrative. The paper does not establish how common shadow governance is or a sharp threshold at which it stabilizes. Retroactive legitimization can itself become a rubber stamp.

**Post-publication relationship note:** later formal work on history-bound emergency settlement suggests a possible counter-design to semantic erasure, but Paper 19 does not test or inherit that design; doing so would be successor work.
