# Plain-language summary

**Companion to:** *The Second Law of Organizations: How Temporal Lag Drives Irreversible Institutional Decay*

**Artifact:** version 1.0 · preprint

**Reading status:** This is an explanatory companion to the paper, not the canonical published artifact. It does not silently amend the version of record and introduces no new paper claims.

Organizations can appear healthy for years and then degrade rapidly. Familiar explanations—bad leadership, greed, incompetence, or cultural decline—may matter, but they do not by themselves explain the recurring pattern of long quiet periods, sudden transitions, and difficult recovery. The paper asks whether temporal lag between fast operational activity and slow strategic correction can create that pattern even without a new moral failure.

The paper's answer is that delay can become a source of instability in its own right. It models a controller that repeatedly acts on stale conditions and treats the resulting mismatch as effective noise. Under the model's averaging assumptions, that disturbance grows quadratically with lag. It then places the organization in an uneven landscape: high-quality states are narrow and difficult to maintain, while degraded states are broader and easier to remain in. Added disturbance therefore makes departure easier than return.

This framing explains why apparent stability can be misleading. The paper identifies a practical sequence to watch for: a long quiet period, flicker near a boundary, a sudden transition, movement into the broader degraded regime, and hysteresis that makes reversal difficult. Its contribution is not the claim that every institution is a thermodynamic system, but a mechanism for asking whether stale feedback is consuming the margin that keeps a valuable organizational state viable.

## Claim boundary

Institutions are not literal thermodynamic systems; the paper uses thermodynamic mathematics as an analogy. Its simulation is a deliberately simple proof of concept, not a fitted model of any institution, and establishes internal consistency rather than external validation. The quadratic scaling is a leading-order result under stated assumptions. Basin geometry, Markovian dynamics, parameter estimates, and the retrospective cases all require empirical testing. The paper does not claim that lag is the only cause of institutional decay or that human agency is powerless.
